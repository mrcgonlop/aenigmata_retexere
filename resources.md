additional notes:

Your ΑΥΤΥ should be ΑΥΤΟΥ — the diphthong ΟΥ probably looked like a single Υ because the omicron and upsilon can merge when the ink is faded.
Your ΜΑΧΑΙΡΩ should be ΜΑΧΑΙΡΩΝ — you likely missed the final nu. This is a genitive plural ("of swords"), and you'll notice ΞΥΛΩΝ right after it has the same ending. When a word "should" end in -ΩΝ and you're only seeing -Ω, assume a faded nu.
Your ΕΙϹΤΩ should be ΕΙϹ ΤΩΝ — same missing final nu again.
Now for the lexical content, this is where your etymological approach gets interesting:
ΠΑΡΑΓΙΝΕΤΑΙ — present tense, not aorist. This is the "historical present" — Mark suddenly switches to present tense to make the narrative vivid and urgent. He does this constantly, far more than the other Gospel writers. It's a Markan stylistic signature and it's one reason scholars think Mark's Greek reflects oral storytelling. The verb is παρα- (alongside, arriving at) + γίνομαι (to come into being, to happen). LSJ will show you that παραγίνομαι specifically means "to arrive on the scene."
ΞΥΛΩΝ — usually translated "clubs" in this context, but the word ξύλον fundamentally means "wood" or "timber." In LSJ you'll find it covers everything from firewood to a wooden beam to a gallows. It's the same word used later for the cross itself in Acts 5:30. The range of this word across the corpus is remarkable.
ΑΠΟ ΤΩΝ ΑΡ[ΧΙΕΡΕΩΝ] — note that Vaticanus reads ΑΠΟ ("from") here where some later manuscripts have ΠΑΡΑ ("from beside"). This is a minor textual variant, but it's the kind of thing you'll start noticing as you compare your transcription against the Nestle-Aland apparatus.

ΓΡΑΙΙΜΑΤΕΩΝ → ΓΡΑΜΜΑΤΕΩΝ — the familiar double-vertical problem, you read the first Μ as ΙΙ but got the second Μ correctly. Progress.
ΠΡΕΒΥΤΕΡΩΝ → ΠΡΕϹΒΥΤΕΡΩΝ — you missed a lunate sigma. The Ϲ before Β can visually merge into the curve of the beta in this hand.
ΑΥΓΟ → ΑΥΤΟΝ — tau read as gamma (Τ/Γ confusion, similar to Δ/Λ — angular letters whose distinguishing features depend on faded strokes), plus a missing final nu.
ΕΠΑΙϹΕ → ΕΠΑΙϹΕΝ — missing final nu again. You'll notice this is a pattern at line endings, where the scribes sometimes wrote nu as a horizontal stroke above the preceding letter rather than as a full letter. Keep an eye out for that supralinear bar — it means nu.
Everything else is essentially correct, which is remarkable for someone not trained in Greek paleography.
Now, the lexical content here is extraordinary for your project:
ϹΥϹϹΗΜΟΝ (sussēmon) — this word is perfect for your etymological approach. It's ΣΥΝ (together, agreed upon) + ΣΗΜΑ (sign, mark). So it means literally "a co-sign" — a pre-arranged signal between conspirators. In LSJ you'll find it used in military contexts for agreed battle signals. Mark is using tactical language for what Judas did. This word does not appear in Matthew or Luke's versions of this scene — they just describe the kiss without naming the concept of a pre-arranged signal. It's uniquely Markan.
ΚΑΤΕΦΙΛΗϹΕΝ vs ΦΙΛΗϹΩ — notice Mark uses two different forms of the verb. Judas says "whomever I φιλήσω" (plain kiss), but when he actually does it, Mark writes κατεφίλησεν — with the prefix κατα- intensifying the action. This isn't just a kiss, it's an emphatic, demonstrative, prolonged kiss. LSJ will show you that καταφιλέω specifically connotes kissing fervently or repeatedly. The prefix shift from the plan to the execution is a deliberate narrative choice by the author.
ΡΑΒΒΕΙ — another Aramaic loanword, like ΠΑϹΧΑ from earlier. From רַבִּי (rabbi, "my great one / my master"). Mark keeps Aramaic terms that Matthew and Luke tend to replace with Greek equivalents (like διδάσκαλε, "teacher"). This is one reason scholars consider Mark's text closer to an oral Aramaic-speaking milieu.
ΩΤΑΡΙΟΝ — this is a double diminutive, and it's fascinating. The base word is οὖς (ous, "ear"). The first diminutive gives you ὠτίον (ōtion, "little ear"). Then a second diminutive suffix gives you ὠτάριον (ōtarion, "tiny ear"). This kind of stacked diminutive is characteristic of colloquial Koine Greek — everyday spoken language rather than literary prose. It's another marker of Mark's register.

You transcribed ΟΙϹ, but what the manuscript actually has is Ο ΙϹ̅ — that's omicron, then a space, then iota-sigma with a horizontal overline bar. This is the nomen sacrum for ΙΗϹΟΥϹ (Jesus). The scribe abbreviated sacred names by writing just the first and last letters with an overbar. You've already encountered ΤΥ ΘΥ (for ΤΟΥ ΘΕΟΥ, "of God") in your Psalm transcription. Here's the full set you'll see constantly:
ΙϹ̅ = ΙΗϹΟΥϹ (Jesus), ΧϹ̅ = ΧΡΙϹΤΟϹ (Christ), ΘϹ̅ = ΘΕΟϹ (God), ΚϹ̅ = ΚΥΡΙΟϹ (Lord), ΠΝΑ̅ = ΠΝΕΥΜΑ (Spirit), and several others. Learning to spot that overline is essential — it completely changes the reading. Without it, ΟΙϹ is nonsense; with it, Ο ΙϹ̅ means "Jesus."

Now the lexical gold:
ΛΗϹΤΗΝ — this word is critical for your non-theological approach. It's almost always translated "thief" or "robber" in church tradition, but that's misleading. Look this up in LSJ and you'll find that λῃστής specifically means a bandit, brigand, or insurrectionist — someone who uses violence, not a common thief (which would be κλέπτης). Josephus uses λῃστής extensively to describe armed Jewish rebels against Rome. So when Jesus says "as against a λῃστήν you came out" — the word carries political-military weight. He's saying "you came armed as if you were arresting a rebel fighter." This is a word where the standard devotional translation actively obscures the political dimension that the Greek makes obvious.
ϹΥΛΛΑΒΕΙΝ — from ΣΥΝ (together) + ΛΑΜΒΑΝΩ (to take, seize). The compound specifically means "to arrest, to apprehend." It's a legal/military term — the same word used for arresting criminals in Athenian legal texts. Check LSJ under συλλαμβάνω.
ϹΥΝΗΚΟΛΟΥΘΕΙ — another ΣΥΝ- compound, describing the νεανίσκος. It's ΣΥΝ (together with) + ΑΚΟΛΟΥΘΕΩ (to follow). The imperfect tense means "was following along with" — continuous action. This is a different word from the standard ΑΚΟΛΟΥΘΕΩ that Mark uses for the disciples following Jesus. The ΣΥΝ- prefix implies close accompaniment, intimacy of movement. This young man wasn't just trailing behind — he was keeping pace alongside.
